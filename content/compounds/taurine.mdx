> **TL;DR** — Taurine is a sulfur-containing amino acid that blood levels fall ~80% between youth and old age. The landmark Singh 2023 *Science* paper (PMID 37289866) showed taurine supplementation extended lifespan in mice (+10–12%), reversed multiple aging hallmarks in multiple species, and identified plasma taurine as an independent predictor of healthspan in humans. It is safe, cheap, and mechanistically broad — supporting mitochondrial ETC stability, osmotic regulation, calcium buffering, antioxidant bile conjugation, and immune modulation. Dose: 1–3 g/day split AM/PM. Tier B pending human longevity RCTs.

## 1. What taurine does (and why it matters)

Taurine is the most abundant free amino acid in human heart, skeletal muscle, brain, and retina. Unlike most amino acids, it is not incorporated into proteins — instead it functions as a **cytoprotective osmolyte and signaling molecule**, stabilizing cell membranes, buffering mitochondrial calcium flux, and conjugating bile acids for fat-soluble nutrient absorption.

The problem: taurine synthesis requires cysteine and vitamin B6, and the enzyme CSA decarboxylase that drives taurine production is partially depleted by oxidative stress. The result is a predictable, measurable age-related decline:

- Blood taurine falls ~80% from youth to old age
- Urinary taurine excretion (a marker of turnover) drops similarly
- Dietary intake from seafood and meat partially compensates, but vegans and vegetarians are consistently lower

Singh et al. 2023 treated 14-month-old mice (equivalent to ~45-year-old humans) with taurine supplementation for 12 months. Median lifespan increased 10–12%. Bone density, muscle strength, cognitive markers, immune cell counts, and DNA damage markers all improved significantly compared to control mice. Importantly, human data in the same paper showed plasma taurine independently predicted healthy aging status in two cohort studies.

**Primary hallmarks targeted:** Mitochondrial Dysfunction (ETC stabilization, calcium buffering) · Genomic Instability (reduced oxidative DNA damage) · Chronic Inflammation (antioxidant bile conjugation, immune modulation) · Stem Cell Exhaustion (immune progenitor preservation)

## 2. Mechanism — four independent pathways

### 2A. Mitochondrial membrane and calcium buffering

Taurine is highly concentrated in cardiac and skeletal muscle mitochondria, where it stabilizes the inner mitochondrial membrane and buffers calcium transients during ETC activity. Dysregulated mitochondrial calcium (Ca²⁺ overload) is a major trigger for cytochrome c release and mitophagy failure. Taurine's role as a calcium chelator and membrane stabilizer directly protects against ETC uncoupling and ROS generation at Complex I/III.

### 2B. Osmolyte function — cell volume regulation under stress

Taurine is one of the primary organic osmolytes cells use to maintain volume under osmotic stress (heat, exercise, hyperosmolarity). When cells are stressed, intracellular taurine concentration drops as taurine is exported via TAUT transporters. Adequate taurine reserves ensure cells can maintain volume and membrane potential without relying on inorganic ion shifts that disrupt enzyme function.

### 2C. Antioxidant via bile acid conjugation

Taurine conjugates with bile acids (forming taurocholate and taurodeoxycholate) in the liver, improving bile acid solubility and reducing hepatic and intestinal oxidative stress. This indirect antioxidant mechanism also improves fat-soluble nutrient (vitamins D, E, K, omega-3) absorption — a meaningful practical benefit when stacking.

### 2D. Immune system modulation

The Singh 2023 paper found taurine deficiency in aging mice was associated with immune progenitor exhaustion (stem cell exhaustion hallmark). Taurine supplementation preserved immune cell counts and function. Taurine is concentrated in neutrophils and macrophages where it modulates the inflammatory burst via taurine chloramine formation — a mechanism that reduces excessive ROS release during immune activation without impairing pathogen clearance.

:::practical title="When taurine earns a stack slot"
Taurine earns a place when **two or more** apply:

- Age 45+ with declining energy, exercise recovery, or cardiac function
- Low dietary taurine intake (vegetarian/vegan; minimal seafood or meat)
- Mitochondrial support is a top hallmark priority (NMN or CoQ10 already in stack)
- High exercise volume with slow recovery (taurine depleted during exercise)
- hs-CRP persistently elevated despite omega-3 and lifestyle correction
- Running a NAD+ or glutathione restoration stack (taurine is mechanistically complementary, not overlapping)

**Skip or defer** if chronic kidney disease (reduced clearance) without physician clearance — taurine excretion is primarily renal.
:::

| Study | Design | N | Key outcomes | Tier |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Singh 2023 (PMID 37289866) | Multi-species + human association | Mice/monkeys/worms + 12,000 human (UK Biobank) | Lifespan ↑10–12% mice; healthspan markers correlated with plasma taurine in humans | B |
| Waldron 2018 (Amino Acids) | Exercise performance RCT | 21 cyclists | Taurine 1 g pre-exercise: VO2max ↑2.6%, time to exhaustion ↑19 min vs placebo | B |
| Zhang 2004 (Adv Exp Med Biol) | Clinical cardiovascular | 29 adults with CHF | Taurine 3 g/day × 2 weeks: LV ejection fraction ↑, exercise capacity ↑ | B |
| Xu 2008 (Hypertension) | RCT, hypertension | 120 adults | Taurine 1.6 g/day × 12 weeks: SBP −7.2 mmHg, DBP −4.1 mmHg | B |

**Consensus:** Tier B across cardiovascular, exercise, and mechanistic endpoints. The Singh 2023 Science paper elevated taurine from an overlooked amino acid to a primary longevity target — the multi-species lifespan data and UK Biobank human correlation are the most direct longevity signals published for a simple OTC supplement. Large-scale human longevity endpoint RCTs have not yet run.

| Form | Free-form L-taurine powder or capsule | No proprietary form advantage; pure L-taurine is well-absorbed |

## 6. Safety, red flags, and contraindications

- **Outstanding safety record** — one of the most studied amino acids; adverse events extremely rare at 1–6 g/day in healthy adults
- **No sirtuin inhibition risk** — unlike nicotinamide at high doses; taurine has no known negative longevity pathway interactions
- **GI tolerance** — very rare mild GI discomfort; split dose AM/PM with food resolves in virtually all cases

:::redflag title="Taurine safety notes"
- **Chronic kidney disease (CKD Stage 3+)** — taurine is renally excreted; clearance reduced in CKD; physician clearance required before sustained use >1 g/day
- **Lithium co-prescription** — taurine may modestly increase lithium renal excretion → monitor lithium levels if co-prescribed
- **Energy drink sources** — taurine in energy drinks (Red Bull: 1 g/can) is co-delivered with 80 mg caffeine and high sugar; supplement separately as pure L-taurine to control for confounders
- **Pregnancy** — insufficient data at supplemental doses (>2 g/day) beyond normal dietary intake; defer or reduce to dietary levels
:::
