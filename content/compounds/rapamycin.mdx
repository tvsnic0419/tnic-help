## 1. Overview

Rapamycin (sirolimus) inhibits **mTORC1** — the nutrient-sensing kinase that prioritizes growth over repair. In genetically heterogeneous mice, rapamycin is the most replicated pharmacological lifespan extension intervention. Human longevity use is **off-label**, prescription-only, and requires physician supervision.

**Primary hallmarks targeted:** Disabled macroautophagy · Deregulated nutrient sensing · Cellular senescence · Mitochondrial dysfunction

:::warning title="Educational module only"
Rapamycin is an immunosuppressant at continuous transplant doses. TNiC documents evidence for informed physician discussions — we do not prescribe, source, or recommend self-medication. See /trust/disclaimers.
:::

:::evidence tier="B"
Harrison 2009 (PMID 19587683): rapamycin started late in life extended median and maximal lifespan in mice. PEARL trial and dog aging studies provide emerging human safety data at lower doses. No completed human longevity RCT.
:::

## 2. Mechanism

### 2.1 mTORC1 inhibition

mTOR Complex 1 integrates signals from insulin, amino acids (leucine), and growth factors. When active:

- **Promotes** protein synthesis, cell growth, lipid biogenesis
- **Suppresses** autophagy, mitophagy, stress resistance

Rapamycin binds FKBP12 → inhibits mTORC1 → de-represses autophagy.

### 2.2 Hallmark connections

| Effect | Mechanism | Hallmark |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Autophagy induction | ULK1 activation when mTORC1 low | Disabled autophagy |
| Senescence modulation | Reduced SASP in some models | Cellular senescence |
| Nutrient sensing reset | Mimics caloric restriction signaling | Nutrient sensing |
| Mitophagy | Enhanced mitochondrial recycling | Mitochondrial dysfunction |

### 2.3 Pulse vs continuous dosing

Mouse longevity data used **continuous** low-dose exposure. Human longevity protocols often use **intermittent/pulse** dosing (e.g., weekly) to reduce immunosuppression while maintaining autophagy benefits. No consensus human protocol exists.

## 3. Evidence summary

| Source | Model | Finding | Tier |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Harrison 2009 (19587683) | Mice (heterogeneous) | ↑ median & maximal lifespan, late-life start effective | Preclinical → B |
| Bjedov 2010 (21276122) | Mice | Replicated lifespan extension | Preclinical |
| PEARL trial | Humans | Safety/pharmacokinetics at low doses | Emerging human |
| Dog Aging Project | Dogs | Mid-life rapamycin safety study | Emerging |
| Mannick 2014 | Humans (elderly) | mTOR inhibition improved immune function (low dose) | B |

**Honest assessment:** Strongest preclinical lifespan drug. Human longevity outcomes unproven. Tier B for mechanistic + emerging human safety; not Tier A until longevity RCT completes.

## 5. Monitoring (physician-managed)

| Test | Frequency | Red flag |
| --- | --- | --- |
| CBC with differential | Every 3 months | WBC <4.0, neutropenia |
| Fasting lipids | Every 3–6 months | Triglycerides >400, LDL spike |
| Fasting glucose / HbA1c | Every 3–6 months | New-onset hyperglycemia |
| Oral mucosal exam | Monthly self-check | Mouth sores (mTOR inhibitor class effect) |
| Infection vigilance | Ongoing | Recurrent infections, slow wound healing |
| LFTs | Baseline, 3 months | Elevated transaminases |

## 6. Safety & contraindications

**Absolute contraindications (discuss with physician):**
- Active infections
- Live vaccines during treatment
- Planned surgery (wound healing impairment)
- Severe hepatic impairment
- Pregnancy

**Drug interactions:** CYP3A4 metabolism — strong inhibitors (azole antifungals) increase levels; inducers decrease efficacy.

**OTC stack note:** NAD+ and antioxidant support does not eliminate rapamycin risks. Physician oversight is mandatory.
