> **TL;DR** — Fisetin is the most potent senolytic flavonoid identified to date — eliminating 25–50% of senescent cells in vitro. Mayo Clinic pilot RCT (2019) confirmed reduced p16/p21 senescence burden and improved physical function in older adults using a pulse-dose protocol (20 mg/kg × 2 consecutive days per month). This is not a daily supplement — pulse dosing is the evidence-based approach. Tier B pending larger RCT.

## 1. What fisetin does (and why it matters)

Senescent cells — "zombie cells" that stop dividing but refuse to die — accumulate with age and secrete the SASP (senescence-associated secretory phenotype), a toxic cocktail of IL-6, IL-8, MMP-3, and other inflammatory mediators that damages surrounding tissue. They are now linked to every major age-related disease. Clearing them is the goal of senolytics.

Fisetin scored the highest senolytic potency of 10 dietary flavonoids screened by the Kirkland lab in 2018 — eliminating 25–50% of senescent cells at physiological concentrations. Unlike the clinical senolytic combination (quercetin + dasatinib, which requires a prescription), fisetin is a consumer-accessible dietary compound with a remarkably clean safety profile.

**Primary hallmarks targeted:** Cellular Senescence · Intercellular Communication (SASP reduction) · Chronic Inflammation

:::evidence tier="B"
Yousefzadeh 2019 (EBioMedicine, PMID 31760212): Mayo Clinic open-label pilot, n=14 adults mean age 72. Fisetin 20 mg/kg × 2 consecutive days per month. Primary outcomes: p16INK4A and p21CIP1 senescence burden in blood and adipose tissue biopsies, plus SPPB composite physical function. Result: significant reductions in both senescence markers and improved physical function score. First human evidence that a dietary senolytic reduces measurable senescent cell burden.
:::

## 2. Mechanism — killing cells that refuse to die

Senescent cells resist apoptosis by upregulating prosurvival pathways (BCL-2, BCL-XL, PI3K/AKT). Senolytics exploit this dependency — they are specifically toxic to senescent cells while leaving healthy cells unaffected. Fisetin achieves this by:

| Pathway | Mechanism | Hallmark link |
| --- | --- | --- |
| BCL-2 inhibition | Destabilizes anti-apoptotic shield in senescent cells | Cellular senescence |
| PI3K/AKT suppression | Removes the survival signal senolytics depend on | Senescence |
| SASP reduction | Clearing senescent cells removes IL-6/IL-8 secretion source | Inflammation |
| Nrf2 activation | Secondary antioxidant pathway engagement | Inflammation |
| p16/p21 clearance | Reduction of cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitors in tissue | Communication |

:::practical title="When fisetin is worth your stack slot"
Fisetin earns a place when **two or more** apply:

- Age ≥45 — senescent cell accumulation accelerates significantly after 40
- Elevated inflammatory markers (hs-CRP >1.0, IL-6 elevated) without clear infectious cause
- Building a senolytic protocol alongside urolithin A (mitophagy) or NMN (SASP suppression)
- Interested in the healthspan-focused Longevity Pro preset

**Skip or defer** if under 35 with no inflammatory signal — senescent cell burden is low; the pulse dose adds minimal benefit relative to the baseline compounds.
:::

| Study | Design | N | Duration | Key outcomes | Tier |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Yousefzadeh 2019 (PMID 31760212) | Mayo Clinic open-label pilot | 14 | 6 months (2-day pulses) | p16/p21 ↓ in blood + adipose, SPPB physical function ↑ | B |
| Kirkland 2018 (PMID 30279143) | In vitro senolytic screen | — | — | Fisetin: highest potency of 10 flavonoids, 25–50% senescent cell elimination | B |
| NCT03675724 | Phase 2 RCT (Mayo, ongoing) | ~70 | 6 months | Primary: physical function, adipose biopsy — results pending | — |
| NCT04733534 | Phase 2 RCT (fisetin + COVID long) | 80 | 6 months | Secondary endpoints for inflammation + cognition — ongoing | — |

**Consensus:** Tier B — human pilot data is genuinely strong for a dietary compound; larger Phase 2 trials underway. The in vitro senolytic ranking and the pilot SPPB/biopsy data are the best human evidence available for a consumer senolytic. Pulse-dose protocol is the evidence basis — not daily supplementation.

## 6. Safety, red flags, and contraindications

- **Clean safety profile** — Mayo pilot reported no serious adverse events in 14 adults over 6 months
- **Mild anticoagulant properties** — inhibits platelet aggregation at high doses via thromboxane suppression

:::redflag title="Do not self-start without clearance"
- **Blood thinners (warfarin, apixaban)** — additive anticoagulation risk at high pulse doses → physician clearance
- **Pre-surgery** — stop 2 weeks before any procedure due to mild anticoagulant effect
- **Chemotherapy** — senolytic activity may interact with treatment protocols → oncologist review required
:::
