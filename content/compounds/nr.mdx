> **TL;DR** — Nicotinamide riboside (NR) raises NAD+ in humans across multiple RCTs. TNiC stacks default to NMN, but NR is a fully defensible substitute — especially if your physician prefers the NR literature or you're on a brand you've confirmed with batch COA. Never combine high-dose NR + NMN without physician oversight.

## 1. What NR does (and why NAD+ decline matters)

NAD+ is a universal coenzyme in over 500 enzymatic reactions — from DNA repair (PARP enzymes) to mitochondrial electron transport, circadian rhythm regulation, and sirtuin-mediated gene silencing. After age 40, **NAD+ levels decline ~40–50%** in most tissues, and this decline correlates with reduced mitochondrial efficiency, impaired DNA repair, and accelerating epigenetic drift.

NR (nicotinamide riboside) is a vitamin B3 derivative that enters the NAD+ biosynthesis pathway via the NRK (nicotinamide riboside kinase) enzyme — converting directly to NMN, then to NAD+.

**Primary hallmarks targeted:** Mitochondrial Dysfunction · Genomic Instability · Epigenetic Alterations · Disabled Autophagy · Cellular Senescence

:::evidence tier="A"
Martens et al. 2018 (PMID 29514064): 1000 mg NR/day for 6 weeks elevated whole-blood NAD+ in healthy middle-aged adults. Trammell et al. 2016 and multiple subsequent RCTs confirm human pharmacokinetics. Safety profile acceptable at standard doses.
:::

## 2. NR vs NMN — one extra enzymatic step

```
NR  →[NRK]→  NMN  →[NMNAT]→  NAD+
NMN          →[NMNAT]→  NAD+
```

The key difference is that NMN bypasses the NRK step and enters the salvage pathway one step closer to NAD+. Whether this matters clinically in healthy adults is debated — both raise NAD+ metabolites in RCTs.

| Factor | NR | NMN |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Enzymatic steps to NAD+ | 2 (NRK → NMNAT) | 1 (NMNAT only) |
| Human NAD+ elevation | Confirmed multiple RCTs | Confirmed multiple RCTs |
| TNiC stack integration | Manual substitution | Native in all presets |
| Typical dose | 300–1000 mg/day | 250–500 mg/day |
| NAMPT dependency | No — bypasses NAMPT | No — bypasses NAMPT |
| Cost per effective dose | Moderate–premium | Moderate |

**NAMPT bypass significance:** Both NR and NMN bypass NAMPT (the rate-limiting enzyme in the de novo pathway), which is why both are used as supplements rather than relying on dietary tryptophan conversion — especially since NAMPT activity itself declines with age.

:::practical title="When NR is the right choice"
NR earns its place over NMN when **two or more** apply:

- Your physician prescribed NR or tracks NR literature specifically
- You have a week-4 NAD+ index result confirming your response on NR
- You're on a Tru Niagen or similar batch-tested NR-Cl brand with COA data
- NMN sourcing in your country is unreliable or expensive
- You're substituting into a TNiC stack manually and prefer the Martens protocol

**Skip NR (choose NMN) if:** You're using Stack Architect natively and don't want to override presets, you're matching the Igarashi 2022 protocol (250 mg NMN), or your physician is neutral.
:::

## 3. Evidence summary

| Study | Molecule | Design | N | Dose | Key outcomes | Tier |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Martens 2018 (PMID 29514064) | NR | RCT, double-blind | 120 | 1000 mg | ↑ whole-blood NAD+, well-tolerated | A |
| Trammell 2016 | NR | RCT, crossover | 12 | 100–1000 mg | ↑ NAD+ metabolites dose-dependently | A |
| Dollerup 2018 | NR | RCT | 40 | 1000 mg | NAD+ rise, no metabolic effect in non-diabetic obese | A |
| Remie 2020 | NR | RCT | 13 | 1000 mg | NAD+ rise, modest mitochondrial signals | B |
| Igarashi 2022 | NMN | RCT | 25 | 250 mg | ↑ NAD+, functional improvement vs NMN | A |

**Consensus:** Tier A for NAD+ elevation in humans. Biomarker improvements (inflammatory markers, mitochondrial indices) are Tier B — endpoint-powered longevity trials for either precursor do not yet exist. The NMN literature is slightly larger and more recently updated; NR is better represented in the 2016–2019 period.

**What we don't know:** Whether the extra NRK conversion step matters for tissue-specific NAD+ delivery in aging humans, and whether 1000 mg NR is optimal vs 250–500 mg NMN for equivalent effect.

## 4. Dosing protocol

| Parameter | Recommendation | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Dose | 300–1000 mg/day | 300 mg is a reasonable start; 1000 mg matches RCT dosing |
| Form | NR-Cl (nicotinamide riboside chloride) | Avoid "stabilized NR" without identity testing data |
| Timing | AM, consistent daily | Take with or without food — absorption is similar |
| Duration | Minimum 12 weeks before assessing | NAD+ stabilizes over 4–8 weeks |
| Cycling | Not established — continuous dosing used in trials | Optional 5-days-on / 2-days-off if cost is a concern |
| Retest | NAD+ index (Jinfiniti or equivalent) at week 4 and week 12 | Target: confirmed rise vs your baseline |

:::decision title="Should you start NR?"
node | Already on NMN with confirmed NAD+ rise and no issues?
yes | No change needed — do not add NR
no | node | Physician prescribed NR or you prefer NR literature?
  yes | Start NR 300 mg AM, titrate to 1000 mg over 4 weeks
  no | Default to NMN — [NMN deep dive](/library/compounds/nmn)
redflag | Do not combine 500 mg+ NR with 500 mg+ NMN — physician oversight required for any dual-precursor protocol
:::

## 5. Monitoring your NAD+ restoration

Monitoring validates that your dose is working. NAD+ metabolite testing is now consumer-accessible.

| Biomarker | What it tells you | Lab / Method |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Whole-blood NAD+ | Direct confirmation NR → NAD+ conversion | Jinfiniti Intracellular NAD Test |
| NAD+/NADH ratio | Mitochondrial redox state | Research-grade; use whole-blood NAD+ for consumer tracking |
| MeNAM (1-methylnicotinamide) | Downstream methylation — indicates NAD+ catabolism | Urine test; elevated = good turnover |
| hs-CRP | Downstream inflammatory signal | Standard lab panel, track at baseline + 12 weeks |

**Flag for physician:** If NAD+ at week 4 shows no rise at 1000 mg NR, discuss: absorption issue, methyl donor deficiency competing with NNMT pathway, or switch to NMN.

## 6. Safety & interactions

**Generally well-tolerated** at 300–1000 mg/day in trials. No serious adverse events in human RCTs at these doses.

| Consideration | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Methyl donor demand | NR catabolism via NNMT consumes methyl groups; ensure B12, folate, choline are adequate |
| Flushing | NR causes no flush (unlike niacin) — this is a differentiator |
| Nausea | Rare at 1000 mg; split dose AM + PM if needed |
| Cancer | Mechanistic debate re: NAMPT in some cancer contexts — discuss with oncologist if relevant |
| Drug interactions | No established major interactions; theoretical consideration with PARP inhibitors |
| Pregnancy / breastfeeding | Insufficient data — defer |

## 7. Buyer guide — NR verification checklist

NR quality varies significantly. Use these standards before purchasing:

- **Form:** Nicotinamide riboside chloride (NR-Cl) — only confirmed bioavailable form
- **COA (Certificate of Analysis):** Identity test confirming NR-Cl (not degradation products)
- **Purity:** ≥98% by HPLC
- **Third-party testing:** NSF or Informed Sport certification preferred
- **Avoid:** "Stabilized NR," NR in softgels with unclear excipients, blends obscuring NR dose
