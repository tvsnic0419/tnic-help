> **TL;DR** — NR (Nicotinamide Riboside) is a legitimate NAD+ precursor with Tier A human pharmacokinetic data from multiple RCTs. NR raises whole-blood NAD+ metabolites in healthy adults at 300–1000 mg/day. TNiC defaults to NMN for stack integration, but NR is a valid choice if your physician prefers it or you already have confirmed response data. The two compounds reach the same destination (NAD+) via slightly different enzymatic routes. Never stack high-dose NR + NMN simultaneously without oversight.

## 1. What NR does — and how it compares to NMN

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) declines approximately **50% between ages 40 and 60** — a fall linked to mitochondrial dysfunction, impaired DNA repair, SIRT1 silencing, and accelerated SASP production from senescent cells. Restoring NAD+ is one of the most mechanistically well-supported longevity interventions available.

Nicotinamide Riboside (NR) is a naturally occurring form of vitamin B3 found in small amounts in milk and yeast. Unlike niacin (which causes flush) or nicotinamide (which can inhibit sirtuins at high doses), NR is efficiently taken up by cells and converted to NAD+ via the Preiss-Handler salvage pathway:

```
NR → NMN (via NRK1/2 enzymes) → NAD+ → SIRT1/3, PARP, CD38 balance
```

The key difference from NMN: NR requires one additional enzymatic step (nicotinamide riboside kinase, NRK) before becoming NMN. Both ultimately restore NAD+ pools. The practical differences are in bioavailability, dose economics, and stack integration — not in whether NAD+ restoration itself works.

**Primary hallmarks targeted:** Mitochondrial Dysfunction · Genomic Instability (PARP-mediated DNA repair) · Epigenetic Alterations (SIRT1/SIRT3) · Cellular Senescence (SASP suppression via NAD+)

:::evidence tier="A"
Martens 2018 (Cell Metabolism, PMID 29514064): First randomized, placebo-controlled trial in healthy older adults (n=24). NR 1000 mg/day × 6 weeks. Primary outcome: whole-blood NAD+ metabolomes via mass spectrometry. Result: significant NAD+ elevation confirmed in all participants. No serious adverse events.
:::

## 2. NR vs NMN — the honest comparison

Both are legitimate. The choice between them depends on your situation, not marketing.

| Factor | NR | NMN |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Enzymatic steps to NAD+ | 2 (NR → NMN → NAD+) | 1 (NMN → NAD+) |
| Human NAD+ elevation confirmed | Yes (Martens 2018, Trammell 2016, others) | Yes (Igarashi 2022, Yi 2023, others) |
| Dose in trials | 300–1000 mg/day | 250–900 mg/day |
| Research volume | ~20 human trials | ~15 human trials |
| Resveratrol synergy in stack | Same mechanism (SIRT1 activation) | Same mechanism |
| TNiC stack integration | Manual substitution required | Native compound ID |
| Head-to-head comparison | See compare table | See compare table |

**Bottom line:** NMN is TNiC's default because stack integration is native and the dose economics are better at 250–500 mg. NR is a defensible substitute — particularly if your physician is familiar with it through Thorne or Chromadex research.

:::practical title="When NR is the right choice"
Choose NR over NMN when **two or more** apply:

- Your physician prescribed NR and is familiar with the Chromadex/Tru Niagen clinical data
- You have week-4 NAD+ index or whole-blood data confirming response on NR
- You prefer a compound with longer commercial history and more independent replication
- GI tolerance to NMN is poor at effective doses
- NMN from your verified vendor is temporarily unavailable

**Choose NMN** if you are building a fresh stack from TNiC presets — it's the native compound ID and requires no manual substitution.
:::

## 3. Evidence summary

| Study | Design | N | Dose | Duration | Key outcomes | Tier |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Martens 2018 (PMID 29514064) | RCT, placebo-controlled | 24 healthy adults | 1000 mg/day | 6 weeks | Whole-blood NAD+ metabolomes significantly elevated; no AEs | A |
| Trammell 2016 (PMID 27721479) | First-in-human dose-escalation | 12 healthy adults | 100, 300, 1000 mg | Single dose | Dose-dependent NAD+ rise; NR-Cl preferred form for stability | A |
| Elhassan 2019 (Cell Reports) | RCT, muscle tissue | 12 healthy older adults | 1000 mg/day | 21 days | Skeletal muscle NAD+ ↑; no change in mitochondrial function markers in 3 weeks | B |
| Remie 2020 (Cell Metabolism) | RCT | 13 healthy overweight adults | 1000 mg/day | 6 weeks | NAD+ ↑; hepatic and adipose fat oxidation genes upregulated | A |
| Dollerup 2018 (Nat Commun) | RCT | 40 obese adults | 2000 mg/day | 12 weeks | NAD+ ↑; no significant change in insulin sensitivity in this population | B |
| Conze 2019 (Sci Reports) | Safety/PK | 140 healthy adults | 100–1000 mg | Up to 8 weeks | Confirmed safety at all doses; NR-Cl most bioavailable form | A |
| Dellinger 2017 (NPJ Aging) | Preclinical crossover | Mice → biomarker bridging | — | — | NAD+ restoration protected against metabolic decline in aging mice | B |

**Consensus:** Tier A for NAD+ elevation in humans — this is as clean as supplement evidence gets for pharmacokinetics. What we *don't* have: a large Phase 3 RCT with longevity endpoints (healthspan, disease incidence, cognitive decline) as primary outcomes. The mechanistic case for NAD+ in aging is strong; the clinical outcomes in humans at standard doses are still accumulating. This is the same limitation as NMN.

## 4. Dosing protocol

| Parameter | Recommendation | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Dose | 300–500 mg/day (standard) or 1000 mg/day (trial-matched) | Most human trials used 1000 mg; 300–500 mg is a practical entry point |
| Form | Nicotinamide Riboside Chloride (NR-Cl) | NR-Cl is more stable than free-base NR; verify on COA |
| Timing | Morning, with or without food | Consistent daily timing is more important than food state |
| Duration | Minimum 12 weeks before judging response | NAD+ kinetics take weeks; cognitive/energy signals lag further |
| Retest | NAD+ index or whole-blood NAD+ at week 4 | Confirms you're a responder before committing 12-week protocol |
| Cycling | No evidence required | Unlike some compounds, no tolerance data exists; continuous use acceptable |

## 6. Methyl donor consideration — the hidden risk

NAD+ salvage pathway recycling consumes S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) and can deplete methyl donors (folate, B12, choline) if dietary intake is inadequate. This is clinically relevant at doses ≥1000 mg/day over many months.

**Signs of methyl donor depletion:**
- Rising homocysteine (>12 μmol/L) on NAD+ precursor protocol
- Sleep quality changes (methylation supports melatonin synthesis)
- Mood changes (methylation supports dopamine/serotonin metabolism)

**Protocol fix:** Add methylfolate 400–800 mcg + methylcobalamin (B12) 500 mcg + choline 300–500 mg to NAD+ protocols lasting >12 weeks. This is especially important at 1000 mg/day doses.

## 7. Safety, red flags, and contraindications

- **Excellent human safety record** — Conze 2019 confirmed safety across 140 subjects, 8 weeks, up to 1000 mg/day
- **No niacin flush** — unlike nicotinic acid; this is the primary reason to choose NR over cheaper B3 forms
- **No sirtuin inhibition** — unlike high-dose nicotinamide/niacinamide (which block SIRT1 at high concentrations)

:::redflag title="NR safety notes"
- **Concurrent high-dose NMN (>500 mg)** — combining both at high doses may strain methyl donors and is not studied; choose one NAD+ precursor
- **Chemotherapy patients** — NAD+ is consumed in DNA repair; some oncologists prefer to not modulate during treatment → oncologist clearance required
- **Elevated homocysteine at baseline** — address methylation deficiency (B12/folate) before starting NAD+ protocol; starting NR will worsen homocysteine without cofactor support
- **Pregnancy/lactation** — insufficient safety data at supplemental doses; defer
:::

## 8. Buyer guide — what to verify before buying NR

NR quality varies enormously. The Chromadex patent on Niagen (NR-Cl) has resulted in licensing to major brands, but third-party manufacturers sell undisclosed forms of variable stability.

**Verification checklist:**
1. **Form**: Must say "Nicotinamide Riboside Chloride" or "NR-Cl" — free-base NR degrades faster in humidity
2. **COA from independent lab**: HPLC identity confirmation (NR-Cl purity >98%), heavy metals, microbial limits
3. **Chromadex licensing**: Tru Niagen, Thorne NiaCel, and Life Extension NAD+ Cell Regenerator use licensed Niagen NR-Cl — highest confidence for the active form
4. **Dose per capsule**: Verify vs label claim — 300 mg capsule should contain ≥295 mg NR-Cl by HPLC
5. **Enteric coating**: Not required for NR (unlike some pH-sensitive compounds) — capsule form fine
6. **Storage**: Cool, dry; NR-Cl is more shelf-stable than free-base, but avoid heat
